The Value of ETF Liquidity — Quick Replication

The Value of ETF Liquidity — Quick Replication

Inspired by Khomyn, Putniņš & Zoican (2024), The Review of Financial Studies

Why do more expensive ETFs like SPY or QQQ dominate trading volume?
This project uses CRSP data (2019–2024) to replicate and visualize the liquidity–fee relationship in ETFs.

Background

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) that track the same index often charge different management fees.

For example, SPY, IVV, and VOO all follow the S&P 500, yet SPY’s fee (0.0945%) is three times higher than its competitors (0.03%).

Surprisingly, SPY and QQQ — the more expensive ETFs — have far higher trading volume and market liquidity, attracting short-term investors who value ease of trading more than fees.

This project illustrates that pattern using real market data.

Key Idea

  • The buyer of ETF pay both management fee and trascation cost.
  • ETF with higher liquidity are charged higher because of their attraction to short term investors.
  • More liquid ETF attract more investors and become monopolized.

Validation

Trading Volume Comparison

The plots below show the average daily dollar volume for each ETF from 2019–2024 (CRSP data).

Even though SPY and QQQ have higher fees, they dominate trading activity — their volumes are more than 10x higher than their cheaper competitors.

Liquidity Persistence Across Time

Even after cheaper ETFs like VOO and QQQM entered the market, the older funds (SPY, QQQ) continue to dominate in trading activity.

Their high turnover levels remain stable across years, confirming that ETF liquidity leadership is self-reinforcing and persistent — the “liquidity network effect” in action.

Volatility and Trading Activity

This scatterplot shows the relationship between monthly return volatility (x-axis) and average monthly turnover (y-axis).

Higher market volatility is associated with higher ETF trading activity, especially for SPY and QQQ.

This confirms that in volatile markets, investors use ETFs as liquidity vehicles — trading them instead of individual stocks.

Regression Evidence

To formally test whether higher-fee ETFs have greater trading volume, I ran a simple OLS regression on monthly panel data:

Variable Coefficient t-stat p-value
Intercept 21.43 116.84 0.000
Fee 454.83 2.95 0.003

Result:

  • β > 0 and statistically significant
  • R² = 0.025 (small, but expected given ETF heterogeneity)

Interpretation:

The positive β supports the paper’s main finding:
Liquidity itself carries economic value. Investors willingly pay higher fees for ETFs that trade more efficiently, reflecting a liquidity rent that accrues to high-turnover ETFs like SPY and QQQ.

Thoughts & Interpretation

  1. Long-Term Investors: Don’t overpay for liquidity.

    For buy-and-hold investors with multi-year horizons, SPY’s superior liquidity provides little benefit.
    Over time, lower management fees (e.g., VOO or IVV) dominate any marginal trading advantage.
    Liquidity is a friend to short-term traders—but often an unnecessary cost for long-term holders.

  2. Short-Term Traders / Strategy Executors: Liquidity is a “tool asset.”

    High-liquidity ETFs like SPY and QQQ allow large trades with minimal price impact, making them ideal instruments for hedging, arbitrage, and tactical allocation. Traders are effectively paying a small “liquidity rent” via higher fees for smoother execution.

  3. Professional Analysts and Allocators: ETF competition is not fully efficient.

    Fee differences persist not because of tracking or brand, but because liquidity creates a natural barrier to competition. Allocators should evaluate both cost and expected liquidity benefits when selecting ETFs for tactical vs strategic exposures.
