Volatility Targeting Is Trendy — Quick Replication

Volatility Targeting Is Trendy — Quick Replication

This post replicates and discusses the paper “Volatility Targeting Is Trendy: How Trend Following Explains Alpha in Volatility-Managed Strategies” by Benjamin T. Hood and Cameron Raughtigan (Parametric, 2024 draft).

Background

The paper investigates why volatility-targeted portfolios seem to outperform simple buy-and-hold strategies. It argues that this excess performance is not an independent “alpha,” but rather the result of implicit exposure to trend following. When controlling for trend exposure, the alpha of volatility-managed equity strategies falls by about two-thirds, with a regression beta of roughly 0.35 to trend signals across global equity index futures.
This trend linkage appears specific to equities, driven by the negative correlation between return direction and volatility—the so-called leverage effect—and does not hold for most commodities, bonds, or FX.

My analysis below follows the authors’ setup to reproduce their key findings and reflect on what this means for practitioners and researchers.

Key Idea and Validation

The paper argues that the alpha from volatility-managed portfolios actually comes from trend-following exposure rather than a true edge.

  • Proof 1: The return of the volatility-targeting strategy can be largely explained by the return of a trend-following strategy.
  • Proof 2: This effect is strongest in equities, where the correlation between volatility and past returns (the leverage effect) is most pronounced.

Even so, volatility targeting remains useful. It stabilizes portfolio risk, reshapes the return distribution, and makes the investment experience more emotionally tolerable for investors.

Empirical Test on Historical Market Data

Annualized Performance

Strategy Ann. Return Ann. Vol Sharpe
Buy-and-Hold (r_bh) 6.42% 17.2% 0.45
Vol-Targeting 8.98% 18.5% 0.56
Simple Trend 6.53% 13.2% 0.55

Interestingly, volatility targeting and trend following deliver similar Sharpe ratios, but volatility targeting achieves higher return and volatility — likely because it employs more leverage and reacts faster to changing risk regimes.

Cumulative log returns of Buy-and-Hold (blue), Vol-Targeting (orange), and Simple Trend (green).

Volatility targeting achieves higher total return and smoother growth, while trend following maintains similar risk-adjusted performance.

Leverage Dynamics

The leverage tends to rise in calm markets and fall sharply during crises (e.g., 2008 and 2020), illustrating how the strategy automatically de-risks in volatile periods.

After controlling for the trend-following return, the alpha almost vanishes and becomes statistically insignificant.
This confirms that most of the volatility-targeting “excess return” is actually explained by its implicit trend exposure.

Interpretation

  • The trend coefficient () implies that the volatility-targeting strategy behaves roughly like a buy-and-hold portfolio with an additional 30% trend-following position.
  • The residual alpha (~0.6%) is no longer statistically significant (p = 0.53).
  • Thus, volatility management doesn’t create new alpha—it repackages trend exposure that already earns a positive risk premium.

My Takeaways

  • Factor-level testing is often sufficient.
    We don’t always need to simulate the entire market; testing on representative factors already reveals key relationships.
  • Understanding market structure matters.
    Knowing that volatility and returns are negatively correlated in equities helps to question whether vol-targeting alpha is genuine.
  • Portfolio design goes beyond expected return.
    Volatility management may not create new alpha, but it shapes the risk experience — reducing drawdowns and making performance smoother.

Note on Scope

This replication focuses on the equity market factor (Mkt-Rf) using publicly available Fama-French data. Due to data availability, I did not extend the tests to other asset classes such as commodities, fixed income, or FX, which in the original paper require proprietary Bloomberg/Refinitiv futures data. Nevertheless, the core finding—that volatility-targeted equity strategies load strongly on trend following—can be fully demonstrated within this dataset.
